Overview

Stress intrudes upon the lives of all people no matter what their background is; it is a nondiscriminating force. It destroys the mental health of its victims and has the ability to affect other parts of daily life, including one's physical health and more. When certain groups such as college students are particularly vulnerable to stress and anxiety, their well-being as a whole is jeopardized. Panic attacks and stressful moments in a person’s life that may be unexpected can constitute a large part of the issue, a problem area we hope to address with our app.

We aim to develop a solution that can help users become more aware, organized, and informed about their own personal stress through visualization and reflection. In doing so, we envision our app to allow users to become noticeably more proactive and optimistic in managing the stress they encounter in their lives.

  • Mental health issues have become widely recognized as a major problem in modern society. Correspondingly, there have been attempts to use technology for improving mental health—primarily in the format of smartphone apps. Here, we categorize some of the existing solutions and analyze their limitations.

    Mindfulness and breathing exercise apps utilize proven biological/neuroscientific mechanisms of reducing anxiety to help the user manage their feelings and behaviors. While they create positive interventions with statistically significant outcomes, they have limitations. They are analogous to a first-aid treatment in that they alleviate the immediate feelings and mental states of users but do not mitigate the root cause of stress or the trigger of anxiety. Second, they utilize anxiety-reducing mechanisms based on human physiology, meaning they are fundamentally non-interactive—the apps cannot choose more effective ways of intervention according to different causes of anxiety. Third, the usage is strictly voluntary—the positive result of the app is directly proportional to user effort, and thus may not help when users are under extreme circumstances, unable to gather motivation or desire to better their conditions.

    Some apps, such as Headspace, do address such limitations by incorporating informative aspects (for long-term improvement) and giving users the ability to choose an exercise from multiple options (providing interactivity/customization).

    Cybertherapy-based apps replicate aspects of conventional mental health counseling. An example is BetterHelp, which functions very similarly to MAHI discussed earlier in class—numerical inputs and text entries from users are reviewed by professionals, who then provide feedback back to users. However, the difference in problem domain raises limitations and concerns that were not present in MAHI: descriptions of feelings and behaviors involve more subtlety in communication than descriptions of diet, which consist largely of factual information, and cannot be fully conveyed as text. Additionally, confidentiality of sensitive information is of particular importance in the space of mental health. The anonymous nature of the internet may cause users to be less assured of confidentiality and reduce the effectiveness of therapy.

    Conventional mental health resources are not categorically similar to our solution. In fact, conventional resources are consistently effective in reducing anxiety and are quite fine as-is. However, personal therapy and mental health facilities are inaccessible to a large portion of the population, due to cultural and financial barriers—which we believe is one of the main reasons why solutions based on personal health informatics are needed. (It is worth mentioning that conventional mental health resources are not perfect, either—the two can be complementary.)

    Personal journaling, again, would normally not be classified into the same category as our solution. However, there is an important similarity: personal journaling serves to provide opportunities for reflection and to promote self-awareness—which is precisely what our app aims to achieve. Theoretically, the insights we will offer to users are in fact achievable by users alone through journaling and introspection; however, the effort required to do so in a complete and thorough manner will be tremendous. Thus, personal journaling demonstrates the burdensomeness of intensive manual tracking, even given its effectiveness.
